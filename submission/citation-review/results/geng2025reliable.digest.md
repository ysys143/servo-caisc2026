## Thesis / Problem

A prerequisite for autonomous AI scientists is the ability to identify the underlying structure of a black-box system from its behavior. The paper studies how well an LLM can **reverse-engineer a black-box function** from data, and crucially contrasts two data-acquisition regimes: **passive observation** (randomly sampled query-response pairs given to the model) versus **active intervention** (the LLM chooses its own queries to probe the black box). The central concern is whether LLMs are "reliable AI scientists," using reverse-engineering as a concrete, controlled proxy for scientific-discovery ability.

## Method

**Task evaluated:** Given a deterministic black-box function f*: X → Y, infer/describe its internal mechanism from a sequence of query-response pairs O = {(x1,y1), ..., (xN,yN)}.

**Two scenarios:**
- **Observation-only:** All queries are randomly sampled from X (uniform); the LLM sees the resulting (x,y) pairs and must produce a hypothesis f = M(O) with no further interaction. This tests inductive reasoning.
- **Observation-intervention:** The LLM is first given a set of observations (|O| = 10 initial), then interacts multi-round. Each round it can: (1) construct a new query x_{N+1} to submit and receive y_{N+1}; (2) construct a candidate (x,y) pair and check its validity against the black box (i.e., 1[y = f*(x)]); or (3) stop and conclude with a hypothesis. Query-response pairs collected during intervention are appended to O for subsequent rounds. Before querying, the LLM may verbalize its current belief/hypothesis.

**What "systematic query access" is given (the intervention interface):** The black box operates in two modes. In intervention mode, the LLM can either (a) submit an input and receive the black box's output, or (b) submit an input-output pair and receive "Correct"/"Incorrect" feedback (Program, Math), or submit a string and be told whether it "is generated by"/"cannot be generated by" the black box (Formal Language). So active access = the LLM designs its own test inputs and gets deterministic feedback.

**Three black-box types (Appendix A):**
1. **Program** — 100 list-mapping program instances from Rule et al. [58]; each is a lambda expression (e.g., `(lambda (singleton (third $0)))`) compiled to Python; input is a list of integers, output is an integer/list.
2. **Formal Language** — 46 distinct formal-language instances (e.g., AⁿBⁿ) from Yang & Piantadosi [79] and McCoy & Griffiths [48]; black box generates valid strings (observation) or validates a submitted string (intervention). Max character length 64.
3. **Math Equation** — Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) utility from economics [21]: U = (Σ aᵢxᵢʳ)^(1/r); LLM queries with two baskets and learns which has higher utility.
(Appendix I adds a fourth exploratory type: **Board Game**, a connect-n variant, with no Bayesian reference.)

**Reference standard:** Bayesian inference models replicated from the original studies ([58], [79], [21]) serve as the "optimal" oracle (MetaProgram Learner for Program; grammar-inference for Formal Language; variational inference on CES for Math). Bayesian model assumes a well-specified prior (true rule is in the hypothesis space).

**Evaluation protocol:** Primarily **descriptive evaluation** — the LLM verbalizes its hypothesis, scored by an LLM judge on a 0–10 scale against the ground truth (Program, Formal Language). For Math Equation, they report **1 − RMSE** between inferred and ground-truth parameters. A secondary **functional evaluation** measures input-output prediction accuracy on held-out test cases (Appendix H).

**Models:** Reverse-engineer LLM = GPT-4o (gpt-4o-2024-08-06); judge = GPT-4o (gpt-4o-2024-05-13); greedy decoding, 3 seeds. Additional models (Appendix E): Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022, DeepSeek-R1, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct.

## Main finding on reliability / failure

**LLMs do NOT reliably reverse-engineer black-box systems from passive observation.** With observation-only data, the LLM's performance **plateaus after ~10 observations** and falls short of Bayesian inference, which keeps improving with more data. **Active intervention helps** — the LLM improves consistently as it collects more self-designed data — **but even with intervention the gap with Bayesian inference is not closed**, "casting doubt on the promise of truly reliable AI scientists." The intervention benefit comes largely from the *process of generating* the interventions (not just the data): intervention data yoked/transferred to another model or fed passively yields lower performance, and is **model-specific / poorly transferable**. Two failure modes identified: **overcomplication** (assumes unwarranted prior knowledge, overly complex hypotheses) and **overlooking** (ignores/underuses observations).

## Scope & explicit limitations (§6)

- The three black-box types "represent only a narrow slice of possible tasks, even within controlled settings"; a comprehensive assessment needs a broader/scaled-up evaluation suite.
- Assumes **idealized, noise-free black boxes** and fully trustworthy data — "rarely met in real scientific practices." Relaxing this (noise/uncertainty robustness) is future work.
- Leaves open: **"How can we train LLMs to become effective reverse engineers?"** (e.g., RL using black-box environments; whether domain improvement generalizes).
- **Limited transferability** of actively acquired data between LLMs is flagged as a concern for multi-agent scientific automation.

## Does NOT claim / boundaries

- Does **not** claim LLMs succeed at reverse-engineering; the headline is that they **fail** to fully utilize passive observations and, even with intervention, **do not close the gap** with Bayesian inference. Reliability is questioned, not demonstrated.
- Does **not** benchmark real-world scientific discovery or full autonomous-scientist pipelines; explicitly uses "simple and controllable black boxes" to isolate core LLM capability, contrasting with complex "LLM scientist" multi-agent systems ([23, 60]).
- Does **not** provide a training method to fix the failures — that is posed as an open question.
- Bayesian "optimality" holds only **under a well-specified prior** (true rule in hypothesis space) — an explicit assumption (App. B).
- Main experiments are **GPT-4o-centric**; other models are in the appendix. The judge is also GPT-4o (LLM-as-judge).
- Board Game is a **counterexample** where intervention does not help — an explicit boundary on the "intervention helps" claim (holds when info signal is dense, not sparse).
- Deterministic black boxes only.
