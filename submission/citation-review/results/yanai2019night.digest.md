# Digest: yanai2019night

**Citation:** Yanai I, Lercher M. "Night science." *Genome Biology* (2019) 20:179. Editorial, Open Access. DOI: 10.1186/s13059-019-1800-6. Received 15 Aug 2019, accepted 23 Aug 2019, published 28 Aug 2019. First of a mini-series.

*(Read BLIND — no manuscript claims known. Based ONLY on this 3-page editorial.)*

## Thesis / Problem

The textbook picture of science ("hypothesis-driven": form a hypothesis, test it against data, discard or modify if it disagrees) is not wrong but incomplete — it hides "the most creative and arguably most significant part of our work." Borrowing François Jacob's terms, the authors split scientific work into two modes:

- **Day science** — the structured, rational testing of hypotheses. You falsify hypotheses, observe which are left standing. The "hunter who has a clear picture of what she is pursuing." This is what teachers/news/papers describe as "the scientific method."
- **Night science** — "where we explore the unstructured realm of possible hypotheses, of ideas not yet fully fleshed out." In day science we *falsify* hypotheses; **in night science we *create* them.** It is the creative generation of novel hypotheses, and also of new methodologies.

Their thesis: night science is a real, crucial, but rarely-discussed part of science that deserves conscious study; making it explicit would help train young scientists (and could relieve the depression rooted in the mismatch between the tidy "scientific method" and messy real experience).

## Method

Not empirical — this is a reflective/opinion editorial. It builds its case through: (1) Jacob's day/night distinction (from his autobiography); (2) a personal narrative of the authors' own "failed" Heidelberg project ~15 years prior; (3) a conceptual "popping-out model" (Fig. 1); (4) analogies to visual art and music.

### KEY POINT REQUESTED — CONFIRMED

Yes. The editorial explicitly distinguishes **DAY SCIENCE (rigorous hypothesis testing/falsification)** from **NIGHT SCIENCE (the creative, exploratory, intuitive generation of NEW hypotheses/ideas)**, and it explicitly frames the *creation* of genuinely new hypotheses/questions as a **distinct, less-formalizable process** that lies outside the rigorous hypothesis-testing framework. Direct support:

- "By focusing on the structured, rational testing of hypotheses through experiments—*day science*—this description leaves out *night science* ... Night science is where we explore the unstructured realm of possible hypotheses, of ideas not yet fully fleshed out. In day science, we falsify hypotheses and observe which are left standing; **in night science, we create them.**"
- "The workings of night science are **rarely discussed, as they seem abstract and less concrete** compared to the logical description of the formal scientific method."
- Jacob (quoted): "**Night science wanders blind. It hesitates, stumbles, recoils, sweats, wakes with a start.** ... a sort of workshop of the possible where what will become the building material of science is worked out."
- Popping-out model (Fig. 1): day thinking proceeds in logical steps reaching only closely related ideas ("isolated valleys"); night science is the "much more open" world where "leaps among ideas are made possible by intuition, associative thinking, unexplained observations, and loosely applied principles from other fields." When a new idea is generated, one pops back into day to test it efficiently.
- On the Heidelberg project: "We had no hypothesis. We had no well-defined problem either. This certainly wasn't the scientific method." — yet it was productive night science.

So: night-science idea/hypothesis generation is distinct from, and precedes, formal day-science testing, and is not clean/rigorous/formalized.

## Scope

Scientific ideation and creativity, framed for a life-sciences / genomics readership (Genome Biology). Conceptual/philosophical framing of how discovery actually happens vs. how it is reported.

## Does NOT claim / Boundaries

- **Not** an empirical study; no data, no measurements, no statistics. It is an opinion editorial.
- Does **not** claim day science / rigorous hypothesis testing is unimportant — both modes are needed; the two alternate ("popping" back and forth).
- Does **not** advocate that all papers be written as diary-style accounts of the actual (messy) process — only that night science deserves explicit study and honest discussion.
- Does **not** formalize night science into a method or algorithm — it explicitly says the workings are abstract, rarely discussed, "far from clean and rigorous."
- Says **nothing** about AI, machines, automation, computational hypothesis generation, or LLMs. The "creativity" discussed is human. (No claim about whether machines can do night science.)
- Does **not** claim night science is superior to day science — they are complementary "distinct frames of mind."
