## Thesis / Problem (what the textbook covers)

This is an authoritative graduate-level monograph synthesizing the theory of Bayesian nonparametrics: prior construction, computation, and — the primary theoretical focus — the **large-sample (frequentist) behavior of posterior distributions** for infinite-dimensional models. Per the preface (p. xxi), a "fairly complete theory of the large sample behavior of the posterior distribution for dominated experiments" is developed, "covering posterior consistency, contraction rates, adaptation, and distributional approximations," building on "the seminal work of Lorraine Schwartz published in 1965." The back-cover blurb states the book gives "**precise sufficient conditions ... with complete proofs, that ensure desirable posterior properties and behavior.**" It is explicitly the standard reference in this area.

The framework is frequentist: data are assumed generated by a "true" distribution and the question is whether/how well the posterior recovers that data-generating mechanism as sample size → ∞.

## TARGETED FINDING (the load-bearing question)

**YES — the book presents the STANDARD SUFFICIENT CONDITIONS for Bayesian posterior consistency, and it is a standard reference for exactly this.** Posterior consistency is the subject of **Chapter 6 ("Consistency: General Theory")**, and the canonical result — **Schwartz's theorem** — is stated and proved in **Section 6.4**.

The book gives Schwartz's theorem in its classical two-condition form:

> **Theorem 6.16 (Schwartz), p. 138.** "If p₀ ∈ KL(Π) and for every neighborhood U of p₀ there exist tests φₙ such that P₀ⁿφₙ → 0 and sup_{p∈Uᶜ} Pⁿ(1−φₙ) → 0, then the posterior distribution Πₙ(·|X₁,…,Xₙ) in the model X₁,…,Xₙ | p iid∼ p and p ∼ Π is strongly consistent at p₀."

The surrounding prose (p. 138) states the sufficient conditions in words explicitly:

> "Schwartz's theorem is the basic result on posterior consistency for dominated models. **It has two conditions: the true density p₀ should belong to the Kullback-Leibler support of the prior, and the hypothesis p = p₀ should be testable against complements of neighborhoods of p₀.** The first is clearly a Bayesian condition, but the second may be considered a condition to enable recovery of p₀ by any statistical method."

The KL-support condition (prior positivity on KL neighborhoods) is formalized just above the theorem:

> **Definition 6.15 (Kullback-Leibler property), p. 138.** "A density p₀ is said [to] possess the Kullback-Leibler property relative to a prior Π if Π(p: K(p₀; p) < ε) > 0 for every ε > 0. This is denoted p₀ ∈ KL(Π)..." — where K(p₀; p) = ∫ p₀ log(p₀/p) dν.

Introductory framing (p. 137–138): "**A key condition for posterior consistency is that the prior assigns positive probability to any Kullback-Leibler (or KL) neighborhood of the true density.**"

An extended/strengthened version is also given (Theorem 6.17, "Extended Schwartz," p. 138–139) that adds a prior-mass sieve condition ((Pₙ,₂) ≤ e^{−Cn}) to widen applicability. This is the version most often applied in practice, but the classical Theorem 6.16 is the standard "prior KL positivity + tests" statement.

**Conclusion:** This textbook is a correct, standard, authoritative citation for "standard sufficient conditions for Bayesian posterior consistency (Schwartz's theorem: KL support of the prior + existence of exponentially consistent tests)." The relevant location is Chapter 6, specifically Section 6.4 (Theorem 6.16, p. 138).

## Scope / Caveats

- **Domain:** Frequentist large-sample analysis of Bayesian posteriors for **dominated** nonparametric models (iid replicated observations in the base result; extended to general/Markov observations later in Ch. 6).
- The result is about **sufficient** conditions; the theorem is a sufficiency statement, not a characterization. Schwartz's original form "has limited applicability" (p. 138), which is why the extended sieve/entropy versions and Chapter 8 (contraction rates) exist.
- The KL-positivity condition alone (mere positiveness) is described as making "Schwartz's theory unnecessarily weak" for rates — for rate results (Ch. 8) one must quantify the amount of prior mass near p₀ (Remark 6.18, p. 140). But for *consistency* (the question here), KL support + tests is exactly the standard sufficient set.
