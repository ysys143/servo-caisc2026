# Digest: leiden2026

**Title:** Leiden Declaration on Artificial Intelligence and Mathematics
**Date:** June 2026 (reflects AI technologies & mathematical practice "as of May 2026")
**DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.20302944
**Source PDF:** ai_scientist/2_Domain_Applications/Mathematics/Leiden Declaration on AI and Mathematics.pdf (11 pages)
**Convener/contact:** Jim Portegies (Eindhoven University of Technology)
**Sign-on site:** https://leidendeclaration.ai (signing authenticated via ORCID)

## Thesis / Problem
A community declaration calling for action to address the challenges that AI (both symbolic and neural methods for generating and formalizing mathematics) poses to mathematical research. It argues AI threatens five characteristic values of mathematics — (1) proof as the highest-certainty, understanding-conferring activity; (2) attributable authorship with responsibility for correctness; (3) transparent, independently verifiable arguments requiring no proprietary knowledge/equipment; (4) shared standards of depth/difficulty/significance; (5) autonomously shaped expert judgment and research direction. It calls on mathematicians to exercise responsibility for the discipline's continued flourishing and issues actionable recommendations to four audiences.

## Method
Not a research study — it is a normative declaration / call to action. It originated from a September 2025 Lorentz Center conference ("Mechanization and Mathematical Research") at Leiden University; a smaller working group then drafted the Declaration over the following ~8 months "with extensive feedback from the mathematical community." Deliverable = a preamble ("About our values", "Potential threats") plus four sets of recommendations (individuals, organizations/funders, policymakers, commercial AI). Complements the Uppsala Code of Ethics, DORA (San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment), UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science, and the UK Universal Ethical Code for Scientists.

## Scope & limitations
- Adopts the perspective of mathematical *research*, but states much applies to the broader mathematical sciences, education, mentoring, publishing, funding, science policy, and applied use.
- Explicitly a values-based normative document; recommendations are "brief descriptions of actionable recommendations," inviting professional organizations to endorse and add their own provisions.
- Time-bounded: reflects state of AI + practice "as of May 2026."
- Not peer-reviewed research; makes no empirical measurements, no benchmark results, no dataset.

## Does NOT claim / boundaries
- Does NOT report any count of signatories in the PDF itself.
- Does NOT claim AI proofs are currently reliable; conversely warns they are hard to distinguish from correct ones.
- Does NOT endorse removing human oversight; assigns correctness responsibility "exclusively" to humans.
- Does NOT ban AI use — recommends disclosure, attribution, retained human responsibility, and careful tool choice.
- Does NOT provide technical methods/benchmarks — it is policy/ethics, not a study.
