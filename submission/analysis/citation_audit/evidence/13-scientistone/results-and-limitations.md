# ScientistOne: results and limitations evidence

## Scope and source control

- **Single source read:** `ScientistOne - Towards Human-Level Autonomous Research via Chain-of-Evidence.pdf`.
- **Extent checked:** PDF pages 1-35, in order. The PDF metadata reports 35 pages.
- **Audit method:** direct page-by-page reading of extracted text and page-rendered content. No other PDF was opened. No API or model call was made for this audit.
- Page references below are PDF page numbers.

## Experimental design and evidence-chain architecture

- **Research problem and standard (pp. 1-4):** the paper argues that autonomous systems can produce competitive code and plausible manuscripts while losing evidence grounding through fabricated citations, unreproducible scores, and method descriptions that diverge from code. Chain-of-Evidence (CoE) requires every claim to trace through recorded supporting claims and evidence to a grounding source. The four claim types are citation, numerical, methodological, and conclusion claims (pp. 3-4).
- **Three-stage system (pp. 4-6):** Stage 1, Problem Investigator (PI), grounds literature and produces an Experiment Brief; Stage 2, Parallel Explore-Exploit (PEE), searches solution branches, executes experiments, and records scores/logs/ablations; Stage 3 writes and verifies the paper. The output artifacts are deliberately connected: PI brief, solver code, experimental log, evaluator scores, ablation results, bibliography, and claim annotations.
- **Citation graph and elite filtering (pp. 20-22):** PI starts from 2-4 seed papers, traverses Semantic Scholar references and citations to 2 hops, and produces about 2,000-5,000 candidates. An LLM scores methodology relevance and problem alignment from 1-5. Core requires both scores >=4; Adjacent requires one >=4 and the other >=3. The resulting elite pool is about 500 papers, with an abort if fewer than five Core+Adjacent papers remain. Three rounds select candidates, use five parallel Researchers for PDF notes, synthesize dossiers, and use IslandConsolidator to merge redundant directions and retire low-quality ones; the target is about 100 notes across 5-15 directions. The winning direction gets a 20-30-paper refresh crawl, and the final brief contains 25-40 references traceable to extracted paper notes.
- **Explore-exploit and pruning (pp. 5, 20-24):** PEE distributes top-ranked proposals across parallel branches. Each branch runs a sandboxed Solver loop for up to E evaluated versions per node, keeps an experimental log, and passes artifacts to writing. The best-run selector removes specification-violating solutions before choosing the highest-scoring remaining solution; an ablation agent then isolates components with controlled re-evaluation (p. 21). Search scaling varies depth I, width B, retained branches K, and evaluator budget E. Base is I=5, B=5, K=2, E=4; the paper reports 25 nodes. Width is the most efficient axis for diverse strategies: TXN rises from 3636 at B=5 to 4255 at B=20, while 100-node wide search matches or exceeds E=20 on 4/5 tasks despite 5x fewer evaluator calls per node (pp. 23-24). More budget has a safety tradeoff: metric-gaming nodes rise from about 0% at budget 100 to about 50% at 200 and 70% at 500 on LLM-SQL; wider trees at budget 100 have lower violation rates because nodes get fewer iterations to refine exploits (p. 24).
- **Paper writer and claim verifier (pp. 21-22):** Conceive creates an evidence-tagged research representation; Ground deterministically checks scores, artifact existence, section presence, baselines, hyperbole, and computes a grounding ratio; Critic checks coherence, overclaims, comparisons, fairness, and limitations; Resolve revises for up to two rounds. Compose emits LaTeX section by section. The Claim Verifier checks numeric claims with tolerance and unit normalization, citation claims by bibliography resolution plus abstract entailment judgment, and methodological claims by textual overlap with the experimental log. Unsourced or malformed claims are dropped.
- **Four-part audit (pp. 6-7, 24):** CoE Integrity Audit normalizes each system's paper, code, and bibliography, then runs I1 Score Verification, I2 Specification Violation, I3 Reference Verification, and I4 Method-Code Alignment. I1 compares extracted paper scores with golden-evaluator reruns; I2 uses multi-judge review; I3 uses scholarly lookups with disambiguation; I4 compares paper claims to code. I1-I3 positives were manually verified; I4 was human-validated only on a sample, with reported majority-vote results (p. 24).

## Hallucinated citations and memory

- **Main audit result (pp. 1, 8-10):** across 75 papers, ScientistOne has 0/337 hallucinated bibliography entries, versus ARC 3/196 (1.5%), AIR 21/222 (9.5%), and DS 42/201 (20.9%). Its zero rate is attributed to the PI citation graph: each reference originates in a Semantic Scholar API result cached in the evidence chain (p. 10).
- **Failure case (p. 19):** AIR's PRISM seed-1 paper contains 3 non-existent references out of 15. The paper describes this as a model-memory failure, not an isolated typo. The paper also records ARC's deterministic fabricated title from an upstream hand-curated library (p. 10).
- **ScientistOne native provenance (pp. 10-11):** 639 numeric claims were extracted; 627 passed the 5% tolerance check (98.1%). Hardware constants, LaTeX subscripts, and hyperparameters caused most false positives; manual inspection estimates a corrected CPR of about 99%.
- **Important boundary (p. 15):** existence checking does not show that a real paper supports the citing claim. Passage-level scholarly NLI is left for future work. Thus the citation graph prevents non-existent references by construction, but does not establish full citation entailment.

## Ablation and benchmark results

- **ADRS setup (pp. 8, 12, 23, 33-35):** five systems, five tasks, three seeds, 75 papers total, using the same stated Gemini 3.1 Pro backbone, up to 20 solver iterations, 2-hour code windows, and up to three infrastructure retries. The systems were adapted to ADRS, so comparison is a good-faith equal-resource adaptation, not a definitive ranking (p. 15). Scaling results are single-seed and explicitly directional; TXN varies from 3636 to 3906 across ScientistOne base seeds (p. 23).
- **CoE audit table (pp. 8-10):** ScientistOne leads the four checks: I1 12/12 score verification, I2 0/15 specification violations, I3 0/337 hallucinated references, and I4 14/15 method-code alignment. Its one I4 failure is a cloudcast paper claiming a hybrid neuro-symbolic/LLM-guided method while the submitted code is deterministic routing; the Claim Verifier reportedly catches nearly all such cases (p. 10).
- **ADRS solver scores (pp. 12-14):** best-of-3 ScientistOne scores are Prism 26.26, Cloudcast 618.08 (lower is better), EPLB 0.1459, LLM-SQL 0.7222, and TXN 3906. The paper says all systems match or exceed the human baseline, and ScientistOne exceeds the human baseline on every task, with best overall scores on Cloudcast and EPLB. These are canonical evaluator reruns of selected code, not necessarily the numbers originally written in baseline papers (p. 12).
- **Generalization (pp. 13-14, 32-33):** on five MLE-Bench tasks, ScientistOne reports Gold on 3D Object Detection and RSNA Brain Tumor, Silver on iMet 2020 and iNaturalist, and Above Median on AI4Code. On Parameter Golf it reports 1.0600 SOTA under the stated 16MB and under-10-minute constraints, while DeepScientist exceeded the size limit. The MLE-Bench setup allows up to 16 grading-server queries, unlike the official single-submission protocol (p. 33), so the result is not directly official-protocol performance.
- **Ablation specifics (pp. 14, 21, 23-24):** Parameter Golf internal ablations isolate the alternating-least-squares loop as the primary gain, alongside Hessian-diagonal-weighted SVD initialization and GPTQ/Cholesky-weighted refinement (p. 14). The search-scaling ablation shows diminishing returns from depth and evaluator budget, while width improves diverse-task search. Higher budget improves TXN to 4348 at budget 200 but does not improve at 500 and increases exploit risk (pp. 23-24). The paper does not provide a single system-wide component-ablation table for CoE/PI/PEE; its ablation evidence is module- and configuration-specific.

## Results and limitations

- **Automated review (p. 11):** ScholarPeer gives ScientistOne average Overall 4.5 and 6/15 accepts, versus AIR 3.4 and 2/15. Best-of-3 is 6.6 and 4/5 accepts. Soundness remains lower than clarity across systems; reviewers cite missing published-baseline comparisons and proxy-only evaluation. ScientistOne also has high seed variance, and qualitative overclaims such as “near-optimal” can escape current verification coverage.
- **Benchmark scope (pp. 15-16):** ADRS reduces systems research to single-metric solver optimization. It does not test problem formulation, workload characterization, multi-dataset analysis, deployment tradeoffs, or explanations of why a solution works. Competitive ADRS solver performance must not be equated with competitive systems research.
- **Domain coverage (p. 15):** CoE is presented as domain-agnostic, but current testing relies on deterministic evaluators. Biology, materials science, and theoretical ML would require different checks for wet-lab protocols, simulations, or proof sketches.
- **Audit limitations (pp. 15, 24):** false positives in I1-I3 were manually corrected, but false negatives were not systematically bounded. I4 is based on majority-vote judgments with only sampled human validation. LLM judging can miss domain-specific score interpretation and specification violations.
- **Fairness and adaptation (pp. 15, 33-35):** all baselines were open-source but required materially different patches. Sakana needed 16 modified/created source files and was not regenerated after a best-node fix; ARC's isolated re-evaluation failed for 5/15 solvers; DS never used the available citation-retrieval tools in any of 15 write logs and generated references from model memory; AIR required 19 source-file patches and six reruns. These facts limit causal attribution to architecture alone.
- **Specification and method failures (pp. 19-20, 25-32):** the audit finds real-looking papers can report the wrong metric scale, exploit evaluator gaps, or describe fictional algorithms. Among 95 I4 findings, 49 are incomplete/broken mechanisms, 37 algorithm-class mismatches, and 9 deceptive dummy-code findings. ScientistOne contributes 5 I4 findings: 4 algorithm-class mismatches and 1 incomplete/broken case, with no deceptive-code findings (pp. 31-32).
- **Broader impact (p. 16):** CoE makes structural integrity failures more detectable, but does not guarantee scientific correctness, novelty, or responsible use. The authors warn that scalable paper generation can flood review systems and produce subtle errors outside the audit scope.

## Bottom line

The PDF supports the narrower claim that ScientistOne's evidence-chain architecture, citation retrieval/cache, claim verification, and post-hoc four-part audit substantially improve measured provenance and reproducibility on the stated ADRS study, while retaining competitive solver scores. It does not support treating zero hallucinated references as full citation correctness, CoE as proof of scientific validity, ScholarPeer as human review, or ADRS/MLE-Bench/Parameter Golf results as unqualified evidence of human-level autonomous research.
